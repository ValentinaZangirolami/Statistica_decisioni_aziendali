Dataset: ESG ratings

ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) refers to a set of criteria used to evaluate a companys sustainability and ethical impact:

  • Environmental Impact on the natural environment (climate change, pollution, resource use).

  • Social Relationships with people (employees, communities, customers) focusing on diversity and human rights.

  • Governance Leadership ethics, transparency, shareholder rights, and anti-corruption policies in corporate decision-making.

ESG helps investors and customers assess whether a business operates responsibly for long-term success. The main ESG metric is the Overall ESG score which is computed as a weighted sum of Environmental score, Social score and Governance score.

There are 13 variables:

  • Industry: Sector of the company

  • Region: Region of the company

  • Revenue: Annual revenue in millions USD

  • ProfitMargin: Net profit margin as percentage of revenue

  • MarketCap: Market capitalization in millions USD

  • GrowthRate: Year-over-year revenue growth rate (%)

  • ESG_Overall: Aggregate ESG sustainability score (0100)

  • ESG_Environmental: Environmental sustainability score (0100)

  • ESG_Social: Social responsibility score (0100)

  • ESG_Governance: Corporate governance quality score (0100)

  • CarbonEmissions: Annual carbon emissions in tons \(CO_2\)

  • WaterUsage: Annual water usage in cubic meters

  • EnergyConsumption: Annual energy consumption in megawatt-hours (MWh)

We exclude some variables to reduce the correlation among covariates (ESG Overall is generally a weighted sum of the other scores) and simplify the problem.

cat_variables <- esg_data |> select(Industry)
esg_data <- esg_data |> select(-c(Industry, Region, ProfitMargin, ESG_Environmental, ESG_Social, ESG_Governance, EnergyConsumption, WaterUsage, CarbonEmissions))

Therefore, an appropriate regression model is represented by

\[MarketCap_i = \beta_1 + \beta_2 Revenue_i + \beta_3 GrowthRate_i + \beta_4 ESG\_Overall_i + \epsilon_i\] with the following assumptions:

  • \(\epsilon_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} N(0, \sigma^2)\), \(i=1, \ldots, 4000\)

  • Linear independence of covariates (Here, it is challenging)

Question: In the case of \(k \ge 1\) covariates, how can we show graphically the relationship between the response variable Total and our covariates?

Answer: When we have 2 covariates, the right plot should be the 3-dimensional plot. However, the main issue is the lower intepretability and it is just available in the case of 2 covariates. One way would be to represent our data through a Scatter matrix, keeping in mind we are just considering the marginal effect of each independent variable on the response (although with multiple regression we want to explain the combined effect). But it can be used with \(k \ge 2\) covariates.

Marginal interpretation

  • MarketCap vs Revenue: the relationship among these two variables appears to be linear. Indeed, the linear correlation is really high.

  • MarketCap vs GrowthRate: the relationship is not properly linear and the correlation is low.

  • MarketCap vs ESG_Score: the relationship is not properly linear and the correlation is low.

  • Relationship among independent variables: we can observe that the correlation among independent variables (GrowthRate-Revenue, ESG_Overall-Revenue, HP-ecc.) is not high, which represents a positive aspect in our case. Indeed, a marked linear dependence between independent variables leads to issues such as in the regression matrix X the corresponding columns are almost linearly dependent (multicollinearity). In the presence of multicollinearity, the estimates may be numerically unstable because the matrix \((X^TX)\) is nearly singular (a matrix is invertible iff it is nonsingular). \(\implies\) a good model should not include pairs of highly correlated variables.

The estimated model corresponds to

\[ \widehat{MarketCap}_i = -5885.75 + 3.35 Revenue_i - 34.64 GrowthRate_i + 66.62 ESG\_Overall_i\] - Coefficients: In this section, we have 4 columns (Estimate, Std. Error, t-value, p-value). Estimate refers to the estimates of regression coefficients. Std. Error is the square root of the estimated variance of each estimator. The t-value is the test statistic of t-test with the related p-value.

Considering the following system of hypothesis \[\begin{cases} \mbox{H0: } \beta_r = 0\\ \mbox{H1: } \beta_r \ne 0 \end{cases}\]

for \(r=\{1,2,3,4,5\}\). The t-values for \(\beta_1\), \(\beta_2\), \(\beta_3\) and \(\beta_4\) are \(t_1^{oss} = -3.393\), \(t_2^{oss} = 91.535\), \(t_3^{oss} = -0.69\) and \(t_4^{oss} = 2.254\).

Significance of coefficients: \(\beta_1\) is likely to reject the hypothesis of no-significance. \(\beta_2\) is significant at any level. \(\beta_3\) seems to be not significant. \(\beta_5\) is significant at \(\alpha=0.1\).

  • Goodness of fit: The last part of the output refers to checking the goodness of fit. In this case, the F-test has the following characteristics \[\begin{cases} \mbox{H0: } \beta_2 = \beta_3 =\beta_4 =0\\ \mbox{H1: } \exists j \mbox{ s.t. }\beta_j \ne 0\mbox{, j=2,3,4} \end{cases}\]

The f-value is \(f^{oss} = 2975\) and the p-value is \(\alpha^{oss}= P(F_{3, 3996} > f^{oss}) \approx 0\). We reject the null hypothesis.

The residual standard error corresponds to \(\sqrt{SSE/(n-5)}\) where \(n-5 = 3996\) (degree of freedom).

The coefficients of determination correspond to \(R^2= 0.6907\) and \(R_{(adj)}^2=0.6905\). The adjusted-\(R^2\) is penalized by the number of covariates.

Interpretation Coefficients

  • \(\hat{\beta}_2 = 3.35\), the mean of MarketCap increases by 3.35 millions when Revenue increases by one million while keeping the other independent variables constant

  • \(\hat{\beta}_3 = -34.64\), the mean of MarketCap decreases by 34.64 millions when GrowthRate increases by 1% while keeping the other independent variables constant

  • \(\hat{\beta}_4 = 66.62\), the mean of MarketCap increases by 66.62 millions when ESG_Overall increases by one point-score while keeping the other independent variables constant

Model Selection

Stepwise selection allows to select a model (which can contain a subset of features - thats a way to select features) on the basis of the best value of a criterion.

There are three different ways: (i) Backward selection: starting from the full model, the feature are removed on the basis of a criterion; (ii) Forward selection: starting from the null model, the feature are added (one at a time) on the basis of a criterion; (iii) Both: forward and backward selection, starting from some plausible initial model (not necessarily the simplest or most complex). In each step add or drop one variable that most improves the model.

There main criterion is the Akaike Information Criteria:

\[AIC= -2 \sup \log Lik + 2 p\]

where \(p\) is the number of coefficients.
